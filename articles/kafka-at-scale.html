Kafka at Scale: Lessons from 5 Billion Daily Events

What we learned designing a Kafka topology that handles massive throughput without sacrificing exactly-once guarantees or operational sanity.

Article header image · Replace with <figure><img> tag

A year ago, we were processing roughly 800 million events a day on a fairly standard Kafka setup — a handful of topics, sensible retention, consumer groups that mostly behaved. Then the product launched in three new markets and daily volume crossed 5 billion. The topology that hummed along perfectly at smaller scale started showing cracks.

This is a write-up of what broke, what we changed, and the handful of Kafka design decisions I'd make differently from day one.

The Partition Problem

Our original topic partitioning was designed for a world with 20–30 consumers. Scaling to hundreds of consumer instances revealed that partition count — not broker count — was the actual bottleneck. Kafka's parallelism ceiling is exactly equal to the partition count of a topic, and re-partitioning a live topic without message reordering is genuinely painful.

💡

Key rule: Never go below 12 partitions on any topic that might need to scale. Default to 2× your maximum expected consumer count.

Consumer Group Design

Isolation matters. Separate consumer groups for operational versus analytical workloads — different SLAs, different teams, different alert thresholds. We use group.id naming conventions to make ownership obvious at a glance: payments-ops-v2 vs payments-analytics-v1.

Exactly-Once Is Not Free

Kafka's exactly-once semantics are real, but they come with a latency cost that caught us off guard. The transactional overhead added roughly 35ms to our P99 at peak load.

Know your SLAs before you commit to a consistency model. You cannot bolt on latency requirements after the fact.

— Lesson learned at 3am on a Friday

We adopted idempotent producers plus application-level deduplication for latency-sensitive paths, reserving full transactional producers only where the business strictly required exactly-once. 

from confluent_kafka import Producer

# Idempotent producer — safe for high-throughput, latency-sensitive paths
producer_config = {
    'bootstrap.servers': 'kafka-1:9092,kafka-2:9092,kafka-3:9092',
    'enable.idempotence': True,       # No duplicate messages on retry
    'acks': 'all',                    # Wait for all in-sync replicas
    'retries': 5,
    'max.in.flight.requests.per.connection': 5,
    'compression.type': 'snappy',     # ~3x compression on JSON payloads
}

producer = Producer(producer_config)

def on_delivery(err, msg):
    if err:
        print(f'Delivery failed for {msg.key()}: {err}')
    else:
        print(f'Delivered → {msg.topic()} partition [{msg.partition()}]')

producer.produce(
    topic='payment-events',
    key=str(event.user_id).encode('utf-8'),
    value=event.to_avro(),       # Avro-serialised via Schema Registry
    callback=on_delivery
)
producer.flush(timeout=10)

Operational Learnings

⚠️

Consumer lag is a lagging indicator. By the time it's alarming, the problem is already 10 minutes old. Instrument produce rate and consumer throughput independently.

  • Broker disk is not infinite — treat retention config as an ongoing operational concern, not set-and-forget
  • Schema Registry is non-negotiable at scale — untyped JSON topics are a future incident waiting to happen
  • Test consumer group rebalance behaviour under load before you need to rely on it in production
# Consumer group config — operational vs analytics isolation
operational-consumer:
  group.id: payments-ops-v2
  auto.offset.reset: latest       # Only new events — fast path
  max.poll.interval.ms: 30000
  session.timeout.ms: 10000

analytics-consumer:
  group.id: payments-analytics-v1
  auto.offset.reset: earliest     # Can replay full history
  max.poll.interval.ms: 300000    # Batch processing — loose timeout ok
  session.timeout.ms: 45000

What I'd Do Differently

Over-partition aggressively, use Schema Registry from commit one, instrument produce and consume rates independently, and plan disk capacity before you need it. Schema drift and insufficient partitions are much harder to fix retroactively than broker tuning.
