Kafka at Scale: Lessons from 5 Billion Daily Events

What we learned designing a Kafka topology that handles massive throughput without sacrificing exactly-once guarantees or operational sanity.

Article header image · Replace with actual image

A year ago, we were processing roughly 800 million events a day on a fairly standard Kafka setup — a handful of topics, sensible retention, consumer groups that mostly behaved. Then the product launched in three new markets simultaneously and daily volume crossed 5 billion. The topology that hummed along perfectly at smaller scale started showing cracks.

This is a write-up of what broke, what we changed, and the handful of Kafka design decisions that I'd make differently from day one given what I know now.

The Partition Problem

Our original topic partitioning was designed for a world with 20-30 consumers. Scaling to hundreds of consumer instances revealed that partition count — not broker count — was the actual bottleneck. Kafka's parallelism ceiling is exactly equal to the partition count of a topic, and re-partitioning a live, high-volume topic without message reordering is genuinely painful.

The lesson: partition more aggressively than you think you need to. Partitions are cheap. Re-partitioning at scale is not. We now default to 2× the maximum expected consumer count as a baseline and never go below 12 partitions on any topic that might need to scale.

Consumer Group Design

Isolation matters. We learned this the hard way when a misconfigured analytics consumer started lagging, which triggered our lag-based alerting and caused the on-call team to investigate what turned out to be a non-issue for the operational system. Separate consumer groups for operational versus analytical workloads. Different SLAs, different teams, different alert thresholds.

Exactly-Once Is Not Free

Kafka's exactly-once semantics are real and they work, but they come with a latency cost that caught us off guard. The transactional overhead added roughly 35ms to our P99 at peak load — tolerable for some use cases, not for our fraud pipeline which had a hard 100ms SLA.

We ended up adopting an idempotent-producer-plus-deduplication approach for latency-sensitive paths, reserving full transactional producers for paths where the business strictly required exactly-once. Know your SLAs before you commit to a consistency model.

Operational Learnings

A few other things that only become obvious at genuine scale:

  • Consumer lag is a lagging indicator. By the time lag is alarming, the problem is already 10 minutes old. Instrument produce rate and consumer throughput independently.
  • Broker disk is not infinite. We've seen teams hit disk saturation because retention periods were set and then forgotten. Treat Kafka retention configuration as an ongoing operational concern.
  • Schema Registry is non-negotiable at scale. Enforce Avro or Protobuf schemas from day one. Untyped JSON topics are a future incident waiting to happen.
  • Test your consumer group rebalance behaviour under load. Rebalances at scale can cause cascading delays that look like a system outage to monitoring.

What I'd Do Differently

If I were starting fresh: over-partition aggressively, use Schema Registry from commit one, instrument produce and consume rates rather than just consumer lag, and do explicit capacity planning for disk before you need it. The rest — broker tuning, consumer group topology, replication factors — can be iterated on. Schema drift and insufficient partitions are much harder to fix retroactively.

Kafka at this scale is genuinely reliable infrastructure. The problems we ran into were almost entirely self-inflicted, and they were predictable. Hope this saves someone the same growth pains.
